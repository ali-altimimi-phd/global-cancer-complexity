Methods

Latent Representation

Gene expression data were embedded into a low-dimensional latent space using a variational autoencoder (VAE). Input features consisted of the top 3000 probes selected by variance. The encoder maps each sample to a latent vector, and all subsequent analyses are performed in this learned space.

Tissue-Specific Comparisons

Samples were grouped by tissue and disease state (normal vs tumor). For each tissue, latent representations were partitioned into normal and tumor groups, defining a tissue-specific transition.

Geometric Measures

For each group, covariance matrices were computed and decomposed into eigenvalues. From these, the following measures were derived:

  • Participation Ratio (PR): effective dimensionality

  • Effective Rank (EffRank): spectral uniformity

  • Condition Number (κ): axis dominance

  • Anisotropy: leading eigenvalue fraction

Spatial measures included centroid distance and group radius.

Delta Metrics

For each metric MMM, changes were defined as:

ΔM=Mtumor−MnormalM = M_{} - M_{}ΔM=Mtumor​−Mnormal​

Taxonomy Construction

A geometric taxonomy was defined using the joint behavior of ΔEffRank and ΔPR:

  • Regime A: anisotropic expansion (EffRank ↓, PR ↑)

  • Regime B: compression / canalization (EffRank ↑, PR ↓)

  • Regime C: uniform expansion (EffRank ↑, PR ↑)

  • Regime D: collapse / simplification (EffRank ↓, PR ↓)

Transformation types were defined using centroid distance and magnitude of ΔEffRank.

Dominant Transformation Pattern

Across all comparisons, the dominant regime is anisotropic expansion, characterized by increasing dimensional spread (PR) and decreasing spectral uniformity (EffRank).

This indicates that tumor states expand into additional degrees of freedom, but in a highly uneven manner.

Decoupling of Dimensionality and Spectral Structure

A moderate negative correlation (≈ −0.41) was observed between ΔEffRank and ΔPR, indicating that increases in dimensionality are accompanied by decreased spectral uniformity. This reflects anisotropic expansion rather than uniform dispersion.

Spatial vs Structural Transformation

Centroid displacement and structural change were negatively correlated (≈ −0.47), indicating that:

  • some cancers undergo large spatial shifts with minimal restructuring

  • others exhibit substantial internal reorganization with limited movement

These represent distinct transformation modes.

Secondary Geometric Effects

Changes in condition number (κ) and anisotropy were weakly correlated, suggesting that axis dominance and global anisotropy represent partially independent geometric features.

Summary

Tumor progression in latent space is characterized by:

  • dominant anisotropic expansion

  • decoupling of dimensionality and spectral structure

  • separation of spatial movement and internal reorganization

These findings support a geometric, multidimensional view of cancer complexity rather than a scalar notion of disorder.
